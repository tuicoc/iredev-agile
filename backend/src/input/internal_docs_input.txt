Over the past year we've watched our internal engineering documentation fall behind reality. Code ships every week, but the wiki articles describing how to operate, deploy, or debug each service drift quietly until a new hire opens one and discovers half of it no longer applies.

We'd like to commission a small internal documentation platform that any engineer can use to keep their team's runbooks, architecture notes, and operational playbooks alive. The scope is internal-only — this is not a public knowledge base, not a customer-facing portal, and we are not asking the team to import or migrate anything from our existing systems.

There is, however, a tension that has come up repeatedly in our planning conversations and we have not been able to settle it ourselves.

Our VP of Engineering is firm that an engineer who notices a stale section should be able to fix it in thirty seconds — type a correction, hit save, and the new version is live for everyone immediately. The argument is that any friction at the point of writing is the reason documentation goes stale in the first place. She has been clear that any approval queue or pre-publication review workflow would, in her words, kill the tool before it ships. When an engineer saves a page, the platform must publish that change right away.

Our Head of Security takes the opposite position with equal conviction. He has seen what happens when engineers paste content into shared documents without thinking — credentials, customer identifiers, references to confidential incident reports. He wants every page save to enter a moderation queue before going live, with someone on the security team or a designated reviewer signing off. To him, the risk of a single accidental disclosure outweighs the cost of slower publishing. When an engineer saves a page, the platform must hold that change until a reviewer approves it.

Our principal tech writer sits between the two of them with a third position. She would accept either workflow on the condition that her team's templated guides — the ones used for onboarding new staff and for canonical support runbooks — are kept locked from in-place editing once published, so a single engineer's well-meaning edit cannot break the canonical version everyone routes new hires to. Outside those templated guides she does not hold a strong view on the publication workflow.

A separate disagreement has surfaced around discussion on pages. Our staff engineers want every page to carry a comment thread so technical disagreements can be hashed out next to the content they concern. Our Head of People is firm that the platform must not become a chat surface — she's seen comment threads on internal wikis at previous companies turn into arguments that nobody manages, and she wants discussion to stay in our existing chat tool. Both apply the rule to the same setting: an engineer reading a page wants to leave a remark.

And we have a quieter group whose perspective we'd like the team to keep in mind: new hires arriving on their first week. They generally don't notice the policy choices behind the platform; they only notice whether the page they're reading was last touched yesterday or eighteen months ago. Their experience is downstream of whichever model we land on.

What we hope comes out of this work: documentation that engineers actually keep current, without our security posture eroding to get there, and without the platform sliding into a chat surface. We accept that the workflow choice between immediate publish and gated review, and the question of whether discussion belongs on the page or not, are the central questions — we need the discovery work to surface what each role would and would not accept in practice.
